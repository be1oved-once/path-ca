Why Revision Feels More Difficult Than Studying

Many CA students struggle more with revision than with learning new chapters. Here's the reason — and the fix.

April 2026 7 min read Revision · Memory

You've heard it your whole academic life: revision is easier than learning. You've already seen the material. You already understand it. All you have to do is go over it again. So why does sitting down for revision feel, for so many CA Foundation students, significantly harder than studying something new? Why does opening a "done" chapter feel heavier than starting a fresh one? The answer isn't laziness. It's psychology — and understanding it changes how you approach the final phase entirely.

The Novelty Problem: Your Brain Is Bored

When you study a new topic, your brain is genuinely engaged. There are new concepts to process, new connections to build, new patterns to recognise. This cognitive novelty produces mild interest and attention — the basic mental engagement that makes studying feel meaningful. It's not exciting in a fun sense, but it's active in a cognitive sense.

When you sit down to revise something you've already studied, your brain immediately recognises the material as known. And known material gets processed differently — more shallowly, more automatically, with less engagement. Your brain is essentially saying: "I've seen this. I can skim." The result is a revision session that feels passive and unsatisfying, where the eyes move across pages but nothing is truly landing. It feels like you're doing something. But the cognitive engagement is genuinely lower than it was the first time.

Your brain doesn't distinguish between "I've read this" and "I can retrieve this under pressure." Revision only works when you force that distinction yourself.

The Confidence Paradox

Here's a specific trap that catches CA students in revision. You revise a chapter. As you go through your notes, everything feels familiar. You nod along. You think: "Yes, I know this." The session ends and you feel reasonably confident about the chapter. Then a week later, an exam question appears on that exact chapter and the retrieval is slow, patchy, and uncertain.

What happened? Recognition masquerading as recall. When you read through revision material, your brain experiences recognition — the sensation of familiarity. This feels indistinguishable from knowing. But recognition and recall are completely different cognitive processes. Recognition says: "Yes, I've seen this before." Recall says: "I can reproduce this from memory without prompting." CA Foundation tests recall. Passive revision builds recognition. The gap between them is where many students' marks disappear.

Recognition vs. Recall

Recognition is easy and automatic. Recall is effortful and trained. When revision consists of reading through material, you are primarily exercising recognition — which the exam never tests. When revision consists of testing yourself before looking, you are exercising recall — which is exactly what the exam demands.

Why "One More Read-Through" Almost Never Works

The default revision strategy for most students is re-reading. Go through the notes again. Highlight the important parts again (often re-highlighting what was already highlighted). Read the summaries. Feel the familiarity. Repeat.

This approach has a specific flaw: it is optimised for the wrong outcome. Re-reading optimises for recognition. It tells you what you've seen. It does not tell you what you can actually retrieve when the book is closed, the clock is running, and the examiner's question is asking you to produce rather than recognise. The preparation that feels most productive — the calm, flowing read-through of familiar material — is often the preparation that builds the least actual exam-relevant capability.

What Revision Actually Needs to Do

Effective revision has one job: rebuild your ability to retrieve and apply information under exam conditions. Everything else is secondary. And this job requires active effort, not passive re-exposure. The brain strengthens retrieval pathways through retrieval practice — through the act of trying to remember, not through the act of being reminded.

This means revision should feel harder than re-reading, not easier. If revision feels easy and comfortable, it is probably not building the retrieval strength the exam requires. The difficulty is the point. The effort of trying to recall something before looking at it — and the occasional failure to do so — is what makes the eventual recall stronger and more reliable under pressure.

Practical Revision Approaches That Actually Work

Close the notes before you test. For every topic you "revise," close the book and try to write the key points from memory first. Then check. The gaps you find are exactly what needs another pass. The things you got right need only light reinforcement.

Use questions, not summaries. Instead of reading a summary of partnership accounts, attempt five partnership questions you haven't seen recently. The question forces retrieval. The retrieval is what revision is actually for.

Verbalise it. Try to explain the chapter out loud as if teaching it to someone. This sounds slightly odd but is extraordinarily effective — because speaking requires production, not just recognition, and production reveals exactly which parts of your understanding are solid and which are fuzzy.

Time yourself. Revision under mild time pressure is more cognitively engaging than revision at leisure. Set a 15-minute timer for a specific section. The urgency keeps the brain active in a way that unlimited time never does.

Revision That Wastes Time

  • Re-reading notes from beginning to end
  • Re-highlighting already highlighted material
  • Reading summaries and nodding along
  • Treating familiarity as readiness
  • Long revision sessions without any self-testing

Revision That Builds Recall

  • Close notes and write key points from memory first
  • Attempt questions before reviewing solutions
  • Explain topics aloud without looking at notes
  • Timed revision blocks with specific targets
  • Error analysis after every self-test attempt

Final Reality Check

Revision feels difficult partly because the brain is bored with familiar material, and partly because effective revision — the kind that actually builds retrieval strength — is supposed to be effortful. The discomfort of trying to recall something and failing, then checking and correcting, is the feeling of revision working. The comfortable flow of reading familiar notes is the feeling of revision not working.

Trust the difficulty. The sessions that feel hardest — where you're testing yourself before you feel ready, where you're finding gaps you thought you'd filled — are doing the most work. The sessions that feel smooth and easy are often doing the least.

If revision feels easy, it's probably recognition, not recall. The exam tests recall. Make revision uncomfortable enough to be useful.
